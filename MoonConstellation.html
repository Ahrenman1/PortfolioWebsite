Moon Orbital Constellation

The task assigned to each team was to devise a satellite constellation to be set up around the moon that would provide total coverage of the surface by at least 4 satellites at once. Each team was given creative freedom to model and simulate results, so our team selected MATLAB to verify and validate that our constellation fulfilled the main objectives.

There were many requirements to conform to, including the solar eclipse period and the launch windows, but my main task was to simulate the ground coverage to ensure our constellation operated correctly. The code was developed from scratch using the orbital mechanics equations derived throughout the class, and gives a visual animation of the satellites orbiting about the moon. The moon surface colour changes (shown in animation above) reflect how many satellites can be seen by that point on the surface at any given time (Purple = 6, Light Blue = 5, Red = 4, Black = less than 4). The code could be easily modified to change the semi major axis, eccentricity, prograde/retrograde, no. satellites on each orbit, and all angles of orbit (inclination etc.).

Two other aspects I was responsible included modelling the pertubations into the ground coverage (animation exaggerates pertubations to show them more clearly), and animating the orbit transfer of the satellite from the carrier to the constellation. This can be shown in the above and left animations respectively. The success of my tasks were very reliant on strong communication between other team members. These results were presented, to back up our design decisions, and we were delighted to have defended our constellation succesfully, without any changes needed.

The graphs opposite illustate the total ground coverage by a minimum of 4, 5 and 6 satellites. The left graph is when 3 satellites are present on each orbit, and the right is for 4 on each orbit. The left graph takes several dips in 100% coverage by 4 satellites, hence it fails the objective and reinforces our decision for 4 satellites per orbit. Since the orbit is periodic, there will always be coverage by 4 satellites across the moon with this arrangement. All code was managed using Git and GitHub, with my teammates Duncan Wither and Laurence Donkers.
